Simplifying a common task with an Android UI fix at Google Timer

UX/UI Design, Interaction Design

Time frame: July 2017 to August 2017
Tools used: Sketch, Adobe Creatve Suite, Draw.io

Google’s stock android clock is an all-in-one package: Alarm, local time, timer and stopwatch. I’ve been using the timer for various things from stretching to boiling pasta and found the user experience to be frustrating and monotonous. There’s no way to organize your timers and it’s way too easy to delete an important timer.

Overcoming this design problem meant reducing the time it takes to do a specific task. It meant exploring solutions to the problem while adhering to Google’s design principles and practices. The project was a good way for me to demonstrate my interaction design skills; a design discipline not taught at BCIT and something I had to learn on my own.

Problem

I don’t have time to swipe through all of this


The main problems of the timer were:

  • Lots of swiping required to get to a specific timer (in this case, the one for boiling pasta)
  • Once you get further down, the dots on right don’t really tell you anything
  • The “delete timer” button is poorly positioned and can be easily pressed by accident (as seen in the video)

Competitive Analysis

Google Timer: Not quite the final countdown


Some stock Android users think of Google’s clock app as the be-all and end-all stopwatch and timer app because it already comes installed on their phones, but that isn’t really the case. Some of the timer apps there made by either smaller mobile app firms or independent developers, contain some features that Google’s timer is lacking.

When I look at these apps, I see other people’s translation of Google’s timer and how it served as inspiration for their own unique Digital products which give off a different feel in terms of the user experience.

Solution

A scalable solution that allows users to view all of their timers in a list


Because timers are stored in the app when set, it’s easy for users to lose track of how many timers they have. I studied the competing timer apps thoroughly and came up with a new interaction of micromanaging multiple timers.

Flow chart of the proposed solution I made with Draw.io

Instead of swiping repeatedly to find a specific timer, users will be able to view a list of all their set timers. Timers can be added and deleted either from the list in a more detailed view.

Google recently made a visual update to their clock app for stock Android phones. The purple and red colour palette may have changed to a dark grey and powder blue, but the design problem addressed in this case study at the time it was written still remains. My interface was designed in accordance with these changes.

Reflection

Design challenges can be found in the apps we use everyday


This project was a reminder for me that interface designers should be able to reiterate their creative process into the improvement of existing apps. It gave me some insight as to what agencies and tech startups are looking for when hiring juniors. I predict the task they will give to designers won't be “We have this crazy idea, go build it,” per se, but more along the lines of “Our product is lacking a robust user experience. What can you do to make it better?”

Existing apps are also great sources of creative inspiration and can lead to the conceptualization and development of new apps.
